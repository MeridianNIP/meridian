How Meridian is licensed, what you're allowed to do with it, and when a paid commercial-use license is required.
AT A GLANCE
Meridian is distributed under the Functional Source License 1.1 (FSL-1.1-Apache-2.0). That means: free to install, modify, and operate for your own organization — including production. A separate commercial-use license is offered for companies whose procurement or compliance teams need a formal paid agreement, indemnification, or an SLA. The two are independent: the FSL grant applies to everyone; the commercial license is optional on top.

1. The FSL grant (default — free)

Under FSL-1.1, any company or individual may do the following, at no cost, without contacting the vendor:

  • Install and run Meridian on any number of hosts within your own organization.
  • Modify the source and redeploy internally.
  • Audit, fork, and contribute back.
  • Use it in production for any purpose that is not a competing commercial offering.

The sole FSL restriction: during the "FSL period" (first two years after a release), you may not host Meridian as a service that competes with the vendor's own hosted / managed offering. Two years after each release the grant converts to plain Apache-2.0 for that release, removing the restriction permanently.

Full license text: Open-source licenses page, and in the file LICENSE at the root of the source tree.

2. The commercial-use license (optional — paid)

Some customers — especially enterprises, regulated industries, and government — cannot deploy open-source software internally without a signed paid agreement. For those cases the vendor offers a commercial-use license that provides:

  • A formal written license agreement with a named contracting entity.
  • Indemnification against third-party IP claims arising from the software.
  • A defined support channel with a response-time SLA.
  • Optional priority bug-fix / feature routing.
  • An installable license key that the portal can display to auditors as proof of commercial coverage.

Pricing and tier details are published on the vendor website and in your purchase agreement. The commercial-use license is not required to use Meridian — the FSL grant above is sufficient for most use cases. It exists purely to satisfy compliance / procurement needs.

SEPARATE PROCESS
The commercial-use license is purchased through a separate vendor portal, not from inside this portal. On successful payment the vendor either (a) generates a signed license key that you download and install into Admin → Branding → License security, or (b) emails you the key file for installs that don't have outbound access. The key generation, payment flow, and renewal process are handled entirely by the commercial portal — Meridian only verifies the signature and records the activation.

3. What the installed key actually does

When a commercial-use key is installed, the portal:

  • Displays it on the Admin → Branding → License security panel with expiry countdown.
  • Adds a "commercial coverage" badge that auditors can screenshot.
  • Logs activation / expiry / renewal events to the audit chain.

The key does not gate any functionality — every feature is available under the FSL grant with or without it. The key is evidence of the commercial contract, nothing more.

5. Data, privacy, telemetry

Meridian runs on your own infrastructure. Operational data (scan results, monitor history, audit log, configuration) stays on-box. Outbound traffic from the core portal is limited to:

  • Commercial-license verification and renewal checks, only if you have installed a commercial key and are not running in --airgapped mode.
  • Optional vendor repository access for OS package mirroring.

Installs configured with --airgapped perform none of the above; if you hold a commercial key it is verified entirely offline.
